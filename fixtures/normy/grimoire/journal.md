# Sam's baking journal

Notes I scribble right after a bake, while I still remember what I changed.

## 2026-06-15 — first loaf came out flat

made my first real loaf and it came out like a frisbee. 500g flour, 375g water, 100g starter, 10g salt. the starter wasn't really bubbling, it just kind of sat there, and I used it anyway because I was impatient. downstream everything depends on the starter being awake, and mine was asleep.

open:
- how do I know when the starter is actually ready?

## 2026-06-17 — the float test finally made sense

fed the starter and waited until it doubled, about 6 hours on the counter. dropped a spoonful in water and it floated this time instead of sinking. the loaf actually had holes in it. so the float test isn't a trick, it's just the starter telling me it's full of gas and ready to work.

## 2026-06-19 — I keep tearing the top when I score it

the scoring went badly again. the blade dragged and tore a ragged line instead of cutting clean, and the loaf baked lopsided. the dough was warm and sticky straight off the counter. it feels like I'm fighting the dough instead of working with it.

open:
- why does the blade drag on warm dough?

## 2026-06-22 — cold dough scores clean

put the shaped loaf in the fridge overnight and scored it straight out of the cold. the blade went through like it was butter, no tearing, one clean line, and it opened up way more in the oven. cold dough holds the blade steady. patience is basically an ingredient and I keep forgetting to add it.

## 2026-06-25 — it collapsed when I proofed it too long

got distracted and left the dough proofing about 5 hours on a warm afternoon. it looked huge and puffy so I thought great, it's ready. but the second I scored it it deflated like a sad balloon and baked into a dense brick. the puffiness was a lie, I let all the gas run out.

open:
- how do I tell overproofed from actually ready?

## 2026-06-28 — the poke test gives me a way to read it

learned the poke test. press a wet finger into the dough. if the dent springs back slowly and leaves a little dimple it's ready. springs back fast, needs more time. doesn't spring back at all, it's gone too far. finally something I can feel instead of just staring at the clock and guessing.

## 2026-07-01 — it was never the clock, it's the temperature

figured out why my proofing times are all over the place. my kitchen is cold in the morning and warm by the afternoon, so the same dough behaves completely differently depending on when I start. the four hours the recipe says is for a warm kitchen. the recipe time is a guess, the dough at the temperature it's actually sitting at is the truth.

## 2026-07-04 — best crumb yet, and I think I finally get it

open airy crumb, big shiny holes, proper tang. what was different: an awake starter, watching the dough instead of the clock, a cold night in the fridge, and scoring it cold. every good loaf I've made came down to the same thing — slowing down and reading what's in front of me instead of rushing to the next step.
